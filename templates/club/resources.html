Getting Started with Heritage Tomatoes

Beginner friendly

These three steps will take you from seed packet to sturdy young plants that are ready to grow on.

1. Seed starting

Sow in late February to April for most of the UK. Use a fine seed compost, barely cover the seed, and keep gently moist and warm (18–22°C) until germination.

  • • Use clean pots or modules
  • • Label with variety & date
  • • Provide bright light after germination

2. Potting on

Once seedlings have two true leaves, move them into individual pots with fresh compost. Bury the stem a little deeper to encourage stronger roots.

  • • Avoid waterlogged compost
  • • Turn plants regularly for even growth
  • • Start very light feeding after a few weeks

3. Hardening off

Before plants move outside, give them 7–10 days of “hardening off” – short daily trips outdoors, gradually increasing time to toughen them up.

  • • Shelter from wind at first
  • • Avoid sudden temperature drops
  • • Plant out after the last frosts

UK Tomato Growing Calendar

Every garden is a little different, but this month-by-month outline works well for most of the UK. Adjust slightly for very mild or very cold areas.

January – February

  • • Plan varieties and order seeds
  • • Clean pots, trays, and labels
  • • Check propagators / windowsills

March – April

  • • Main sowing time for most varieties
  • • Pot on seedlings as they grow
  • • Start gentle airflow / ventilation

May

  • • Harden off plants gradually
  • • Plant out under cover earlier
  • • Outdoors after last frost risk

June – July

  • • Tie in and support tall plants
  • • Remove side shoots on cordons
  • • Start regular feeding when fruits set

August – September

  • • Main harvest period
  • • Remove leaves shading ripening fruit
  • • Save seed from favourite fruits

October – December

  • • Clear old plants and compost healthy material
  • • Clean tools, pots, and supports
  • • Make notes for next season

Common Tomato Problems (and Calm Fixes)

A quick, non-panicky cheat sheet for the issues UK tomato growers see most often.

Blight

Brown patches on leaves and stems, often in warm, humid, showery weather.

  • • Remove and bin affected plants (do not compost)
  • • Improve airflow and avoid overhead watering
  • • Consider blight-resistant varieties outdoors

Blossom end rot

Dark, sunken patch at the base of the fruit. Caused by irregular watering, not a disease.

  • • Keep watering steady and even
  • • Mulch pots / beds to reduce drying out
  • • Damaged fruits are safe but unpretty

Yellowing leaves

Lower leaves naturally age and yellow; upper yellowing can mean feed or watering issues.

  • • Remove very tatty lower leaves
  • • Check watering and drainage
  • • Feed regularly once fruits set

Companion Planting & Container Tips

Good companions

These plants play nicely with tomatoes and can help with pests, pollinators, or space.

  • • Basil – classic partner, attracts pollinators
  • • Marigolds – can help distract pests
  • • Lettuce – enjoys shade under tall plants
  • • Spring onions / chives – useful in the kitchen too

Container essentials

Tomatoes are happy in pots, grow bags, and buckets as long as a few basics are right.

  • • Use at least 10–12L of compost per plant
  • • Add slow-release feed or feed weekly
  • • Ensure drainage holes are generous
  • • Raise pots slightly off patios for run-off
